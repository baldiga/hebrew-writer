## 1. Cognitive/Psychological Patterns in Human Writing

### 1.1 How Humans Structure Arguments (Non-Linear, Tangential)

**Writing as a recursive, non-linear process:**
Human writing does not proceed in a straight line from thesis to conclusion. Research from composition studies consistently shows that writing is recursive -- writers loop back, revise earlier ideas, discover new angles mid-paragraph, and allow arguments to emerge through the act of writing itself. The Purdue OWL and other composition authorities emphasize that "writing is the process of discovering and developing your thought, not merely the transcription of already complete thought."

**Tangential thinking as a feature, not a bug:**
Humans naturally digress. A writer making an argument about urban planning might suddenly recall a childhood memory of a neighborhood park, insert a parenthetical about a related book they read, or shift registers mid-argument from analytical to anecdotal. These tangents reflect genuine associative cognition -- the brain's tendency to link ideas through personal experience, emotion, and memory rather than pure logical sequence.

**Non-linear argument structures:**
Research on academic writing acknowledges that while conventional linear structures exist, effective human writing often employs what researchers call "multi-discourse modes" -- shifting between argumentative, narrative, and descriptive registers within a single piece. Studies published in Applied Linguistics found that essays incorporating narrative digressions within argumentative structures can actually score higher because they demonstrate the writer's authentic engagement with the material.

**Key distinction from AI:** AI writing tends toward rigid linearity: thesis statement, supporting point 1, supporting point 2, supporting point 3, conclusion. Human arguments meander, double back, and sometimes arrive at conclusions the writer didn't expect when they started.

### 1.2 Emotional Variation and Inconsistency

**Mood bleeds into prose:**
Research from Cambridge University Press on the psychology of creative writing demonstrates that writers' emotional states directly influence their output. Positive moods affect divergent thinking performance differently than negative moods, and these emotional states shape "the quantity and quality of ideas" generated during writing.

**Emotional inconsistency as a human marker:**
Human writing naturally fluctuates in emotional register -- a writer might begin a paragraph with measured analysis, grow heated mid-sentence, then pull back with self-deprecating humor. This emotional inconsistency is not a flaw; it reflects the genuine experience of a thinking, feeling person wrestling with ideas in real time.

**Writer-within-community model:**
Research published in educational psychology proposes that "motivational beliefs and emotional reactions influence whether students engage in writing, how much effort they put forth, and composing actions. Emotional and affective reactions moderate writers' use of resources from long-term memory and the control and production processes involved in creating text."

### 1.3 Memory-Based References and Personal Anecdotes

**Drawing from semantic memory and lived experience:**
Psycholinguistic research (Arxiv 2505.01800) found that human word selection draws from "semantic memory, lived experiences, and awareness of audience expectations," producing greater vocabulary diversity. In contrast, AI selects words based on "probabilistic associations derived from training data," lacking "lived experience and contextual depth."

**The specificity signal:**
One of the strongest markers of human writing is the inclusion of hyper-specific, seemingly unnecessary details -- the exact color of a grandmother's kitchen curtains, the specific intersection where an accident happened, the brand name of a childhood cereal. These details come from actual memory and serve no argumentative purpose other than grounding the reader in lived reality.

**Why AI cannot replicate this authentically:**
AI can generate fictional anecdotes, but they tend toward the generic ("I remember a time when...") rather than the specific. Research from Northeastern University found that LLMs "tend to omit specific, unusual, nuanced facts and replace them with more generic, positive descriptions" -- a statistical regression to the mean that makes AI anecdotes feel hollow.

### 1.4 Cognitive Biases That Show in Writing

**Confirmation bias:**
Human writers naturally structure arguments around pre-existing beliefs. They tend to "assign more value to -- and try to obtain -- information and ideas that match those we already believe, thereby confirming our existing stance." This manifests as selective evidence presentation, asymmetric treatment of opposing viewpoints, and passionate defense of positions the writer holds personally.

**Anchoring bias:**
Writers tend to "rely too heavily on the first piece of information they receive on a topic," using it as a reference point for subsequent judgments. This shows up as arguments that orbit around an initial framing, even when later evidence might suggest a different frame.

**Availability heuristic:**
Writers disproportionately reference recent events, vivid examples, and personally experienced phenomena over statistically more representative examples.

**Dunning-Kruger patterns:**
Writers display varying levels of confidence that may not correlate with expertise -- overconfidence on topics they know little about, excessive qualification on topics they know well.

**Key distinction from AI:** AI writing tends to present artificially balanced arguments, giving equal weight to all perspectives in a way that no human with genuine beliefs actually does. Humans are biased, and their biases are visible in their writing -- this is paradoxically what makes human writing feel authentic.

### 1.5 How Fatigue, Mood, and Context Affect Writing

**Fatigue effects on writing:**
Research published in PLOS ONE ("Dynamics in typewriting performance reflect mental fatigue during real-life office work") found that fatigue manifests in measurable ways: increased typing errors, longer pauses between words, simplified sentence structures, and reduced vocabulary diversity. These are detectable signals of a human mind tiring during composition.

**Writing anxiety and quality:**
PMC research found "positive correlations between self-efficacy and text quality and negative correlations between writing anxiety and text quality." Writing anxiety produces "fatigue, insomnia, restlessness, and indifference" that leave traces in the text -- shorter paragraphs, more hedging, less ambitious claims.

**Contextual variation:**
A human writing at 2am after three cups of coffee produces detectably different prose than the same human writing at 10am after a good night's sleep. This variation in quality, energy, and register across a document is a fundamentally human signal that AI cannot replicate because AI has no circadian rhythm, no bad days, and no coffee.

### 1.6 "Thinking on Paper" Patterns

**Writing as discovery:**
Educational research consistently finds that "we often don't really know what we think or how we feel about a given subject until we sit down and try to put it into words." This exploratory writing creates distinctive textual artifacts: sentences that begin one direction and end another, paragraphs where the writer visibly changes their mind, and conclusions that contradict or complicate the introduction.

**Discovery drafts:**
The concept of a "discovery draft" -- writing without a predetermined conclusion to find out what you think -- produces writing with a distinctive quality: "the value in a discovery draft is not in the draft itself but in the thinking and creativity that comes from the process."

**Visible reasoning processes:**
Human writing often contains traces of the thinking process: "Well, actually, that's not quite right..." or "I started this paragraph thinking X, but now I realize..." Even in polished prose, vestiges of this exploratory process remain as qualifying phrases, mid-paragraph pivots, and evolving thesis statements.

**Key distinction from AI:** AI begins generating text with complete statistical "knowledge" of how the text should end. It never discovers its own argument. Humans frequently do, and the traces of that discovery are detectable.

## 2. Stylistic Signatures of Human Writing

### 2.1 Burstiness: Alternating Long/Short Sentences

**Definition and measurement:**
Burstiness measures the variance in sentence length and complexity within a text. Research from Originality.AI explains that human writing tends to "burst" with irregular, creative flourishes -- compact, predictable phrases followed by sudden digressions. AI produces "steady output" with "uniform complexity."

**Quantitative findings:**
- AI-generated sentences cluster tightly around **15-20 words per sentence**
- Human writing varies wildly, from **3-word fragments to 40+ word sprawls**
- AI-generated text scores consistently lower on variability scales
- Detection research achieved **over 90% accuracy** identifying AI text through burstiness analysis alone

**Why burstiness matters:**
Human burstiness reflects cognitive states -- a short, punchy sentence often follows a complex one because the writer wants emphasis, or because they're exhausted from the complex thought, or because they sense the reader needs a break. These decisions are intuitive and reflect real-time audience modeling that AI does not perform.

**Example pattern:**
Human: "The infrastructure bill, which had been debated for seventeen months across three congressional sessions and survived two filibusters, finally passed. By one vote. One."
AI equivalent: "The infrastructure bill, which was debated extensively over many months, ultimately passed with a narrow margin in Congress."

### 2.2 Lexical Diversity Patterns

**Type-Token Ratio (TTR):**
Lexical diversity is measured as the ratio of unique words (types) to total words (tokens). Research consistently shows AI text demonstrates "reduced lexical diversity" compared to human writing, with fewer unique vocabulary choices across passages.

**Hapax legomena (words used only once):**
Human writing contains more hapax legomena -- words that appear only once in a text. This reflects the breadth of a human's active vocabulary and their willingness to reach for the unusual word when it fits.

**Function word patterns:**
Function words like "the," "and," and "of" carry little semantic weight but vary in frequency across individual authors. Studies show humans exhibit "idiosyncratic ratios" of function words, while AI produces "unnaturally uniform distributions."

### 2.3 How Humans Use Repetition (Differently Than AI)

**Purposeful human repetition:**
Human writers deploy repetition as a rhetorical device for emphasis, rhythm, and memorability. Examples include anaphora (repeated opening words), epistrophe (repeated closing words), and the kind of deliberate echoing seen in Martin Luther King Jr.'s "I Have a Dream" speech. Writers "edit with purpose, asking: Is this doing work? Does it add emphasis, rhythm, or characterization?"

**AI repetition as redundancy:**
AI produces unintentional redundancy: "the setting serves as a powerful symbol" followed by "the setting serves as a catalyst for the characters' struggles" -- essentially restating the same idea with slight variation. This "looping or padding of content" is a common AI trait, where the model tries to meet length requirements or cover all bases by saying the same thing multiple ways.

**The critical difference:** Human repetition is *intentional and purposeful*; AI repetition is *accidental and redundant*. Human repetition draws attention to itself as a device; AI repetition tries to hide itself as variety.

### 2.4 Imperfect Transitions and Topic Shifts

**Human transitions are often abrupt or absent:**
Humans shift topics through associative leaps that may not be explicitly signaled. A writer might jump from discussing monetary policy to mentioning their uncle's restaurant without a formal transition because the connection exists in their mind even if unstated.

**AI transitions are over-signaled:**
AI relies heavily on formal transitional phrases -- "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally," "In addition" -- appearing 3-5x more frequently than in natural human writing. These create artificial smoothness that paradoxically signals inauthenticity.

**Natural human connectors:**
Humans use conversational connectors: "But," "And," "Plus," "So," "Anyway," "Look," "Here's the thing." These informal transitions reflect actual speech patterns and are far less common in AI output.

### 2.5 Personal Voice and Idiosyncrasy

**Stylometric fingerprinting:**
Every human writer has measurable idiosyncrasies in their prose: characteristic sentence lengths, preferred punctuation, habitual word choices, and distinctive rhythmic patterns. These create what forensic linguists call a "stylometric fingerprint" that is as unique as a physical fingerprint.

**Elements of personal voice:**
- Sentence length preferences and rhythmic patterns
- Preferred punctuation (some writers favor em-dashes, others semicolons, others ellipses)
- Habitual phrases and verbal tics
- Characteristic argument structures
- Preferred register (formal/informal blend)
- Tendency toward certain types of metaphor or analogy

**AI's absence of voice:**
AI maintains a "neutral, detached tone resembling corporate documentation." It lacks "the idiosyncratic preferences and emotional coloring reflecting authentic human perspective and lived experience."

### 2.6 Use of Humor, Sarcasm, Irony

**Humor as an authenticity marker:**
Authentic humor arises from character and context rather than forced insertion. Research on sarcasm in written communication (Oxford Academic) found that sarcasm involves "intentionally using a bitter tone" and "saying something different than what the speaker really means" -- a sophisticated cognitive act requiring theory of mind.

**Why AI struggles with humor:**
AI can pattern-match humor formats but cannot generate genuinely funny content because humor requires:
- Subverting expectations (AI optimizes for expected patterns)
- Shared cultural knowledge and timing
- Willingness to risk offense or confusion
- Authentic personality behind the joke
- Understanding that something is funny (not just that it matches a humor pattern)

**Irony as a human signal:**
Irony requires the writer to say one thing while meaning another, trusting the reader to decode the gap. This trust relationship between writer and reader is fundamentally human.

### 2.7 Specific Details vs. Generic Statements

**The specificity gap:**
One of the most reliable markers of AI writing is its tendency toward generic statements where humans would provide specific details.

| Human Writing | AI Writing |
|---|---|
| "My neighbor Dave, who's 73 and still mows his lawn in jean shorts" | "An elderly neighbor who maintains his property" |
| "The 2019 Boeing 737 MAX groundings" | "Recent aviation safety incidents" |
| "I spent $247 on groceries last Tuesday at Trader Joe's" | "Grocery costs have increased significantly" |
| "She quoted Toni Morrison's 'Beloved' at the funeral" | "Literary references were used during the ceremony" |

**Why AI generalizes:**
LLMs "tend to omit specific, unusual, nuanced facts and replace them with more generic, positive descriptions." This is because the model predicts statistically probable words rather than retrieving specific memories. The result is writing that is technically accurate but informationally hollow.

## 3. What AI Writing Gets Wrong

### 3.1 The "AI Voice" Characteristics

Research identifies the "AI voice" as a distinctive and recognizable register characterized by:

**The "Retail Voice" pattern:**
AI defaults to excessively polished customer-service tones -- "helpful to the point of artificiality, neutral to the point of sterility." This triggers readers' deception detection systems because biological trust "requires friction and vulnerability, not frictionless perfection."

**Hyper-consistency:**
"AI is hyper-consistent -- it is the literary equivalent of a face that is perfectly symmetrical." Humans are erratic, varying sentence length, using slightly odd metaphors, and having a specific cadence to their inner voice.

**The linguistic uncanny valley:**
Like the visual uncanny valley where near-human faces feel creepy, AI text that is almost-but-not-quite human triggers instinctive discomfort. The text "looks human but something crucial is missing." AI "sounds correct instead of true."

**Core deficit -- talking AT vs. talking TO:**
Humans write *to* readers by modeling their reactions and potential misunderstandings. AI "merely calculates statistical word probability." It's "talking *at* you, not *to* you."

### 3.2 Overly Structured/Balanced Arguments

**Five-paragraph essay syndrome:**
AI tends to construct paragraphs with "rigid consistency: a clear topic sentence, followed by supporting evidence, and concluded with a summary statement." While technically sound, this creates text that "feels overly neat and mechanical."

**Artificial balance:**
AI gives equal weight to all perspectives, producing falsely balanced writing. No human with genuine beliefs writes this way -- humans naturally weight arguments toward their own position.

**Predictable structure:**
AI follows predictable formatting: numbered lists, subheadings, and summarizing conclusions. The structural template is visible across different prompts and topics.

### 3.3 Excessive Hedging Phrases

AI exhibits an "excessive reliance on transitional phrases and hedging language that creates an overly cautious tone." Common hedging patterns include:

**Epistemic hedges:** "it's important to note that," "generally speaking," "to some extent," "from a broader perspective," "one could argue that," "it is worth mentioning"

**Safety-trained caution:** These phrases reflect "safety protocols embedded during model training, creating perpetually tentative prose lacking conviction."

**Quantitative evidence:** Instruction-tuned models use hedging constructions **2-5 times more frequently** than humans write naturally.

### 3.4 "Sure, I'd Be Happy To" Patterns

**Conversational artifacts from assistant training:**
AI assistants are trained with RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) to be helpful and agreeable, producing distinctive conversational markers:
- "Sure, I'd be happy to help with that!"
- "Certainly! Let me..."
- "Great question!"
- "Absolutely, here's..."
- "That's a really interesting point."
- "I'd love to help you with..."

These patterns bleed into AI-generated content even when the model isn't in assistant mode, because they are deeply embedded in the model's weights from training.

### 3.5 Uniform Paragraph Lengths

**Measurable uniformity:**
AI generates paragraphs of remarkably similar length -- typically **3-5 sentences, 60-100 words** per paragraph. Human writing varies dramatically, from single-sentence paragraphs used for emphasis to sprawling paragraphs that run half a page.

**Detection signal:**
Paragraph length uniformity is one of the features AI detectors specifically analyze because human paragraph length follows a much wider distribution.

### 3.6 Predictable Transitions

**The transition word problem:**
AI overuses formal transitions at rates 3-5x higher than natural writing:

| AI Transition | Human Alternative |
|---|---|
| "Furthermore" | "And" / "Plus" / (no transition) |
| "Moreover" | "Also" / "On top of that" |
| "Additionally" | "And another thing" / (new paragraph) |
| "Consequently" | "So" |
| "In conclusion" | "Look" / "The point is" |
| "It is worth noting that" | (just state the thing) |
| "Nevertheless" | "But" / "Still" |

### 3.7 Lack of Genuine Opinion/Stance

**The neutrality problem:**
AI "avoids strong opinions or emotional cues unless specifically instructed." It produces writing that "resembles corporate documentation" rather than the work of a thinking person with genuine beliefs.

**Missing stance markers:**
Human writing contains natural opinion markers: "I think," "In my experience," "This is ridiculous," "The obvious answer is." AI defaults to the objective voice, avoiding first-person perspective and personal judgments.

**The risk deficit:**
Humans "occasionally express strong, slightly reckless opinions. Models play it safe." This safety creates writing that is technically balanced but emotionally dead.

### 3.8 Over-Qualification of Statements

AI habitually over-qualifies simple statements:

| What a human would write | What AI writes |
|---|---|
| "Exercise is good for you" | "While individual results may vary based on a multitude of factors, regular physical activity is generally considered to be beneficial for overall health and well-being" |
| "Shakespeare was a great writer" | "William Shakespeare is widely regarded as one of the most influential and accomplished literary figures in the Western literary canon" |
| "This policy failed" | "While there are multiple perspectives on the outcomes of this policy initiative, several indicators suggest that it may not have achieved its intended objectives to the extent originally anticipated" |

### 3.9 Additional AI Voice Problems

**The "rule of three" overuse:**
LLMs overuse the "adjective, adjective, adjective" construction to make superficial analyses appear more comprehensive. Example: "a robust, comprehensive, and innovative approach."

**Em-dash overuse (the "ChatGPT dash"):**
AI uses em-dashes as "universal punctuation" far beyond strategic human applications -- becoming what observers call the "ChatGPT dash."

**Formulaic sentence openers:**
The "From X to Y" construction and present participial phrases (e.g., "Analyzing data, revealing insights") appear with unnatural frequency.

**Artificial enthusiasm:**
AI generates enthusiasm that "feels corporate-reviewed rather than genuine" -- exclamation points and positive framing that lacks the specificity of real excitement.

## 4. Professional Ghostwriting/Shadow Writing Techniques

### 4.1 How Professional Ghostwriters Capture a Client's Voice

**The "method actor" approach:**
Professional ghostwriters operate like method actors: "they study the life experience of their character to understand their values, what experiences shaped their thought patterns, and their perspective on events." The goal is to learn "to think how their author-client thinks, how they process information, and how they view the world."

**Extended immersive interviews:**
- Ghostwriters spend **2-3 hours in initial interviews**, recording every minute
- They study how the client talks about events: "the metaphors they use naturally, how they structure narratives, what details they emphasize, and what emotional tone they bring to different kinds of stories"
- This linguistic data becomes the **voice template** for the entire project
- Multiple follow-up interviews refine the template over weeks or months

**Analyzing existing content:**
- Review all previous written material: articles, blogs, social media posts, emails
- Identify "recurring phrases, sentence structure, and vocabulary choices"
- Study what books and writers the client admires
- Ask: "If your book could sound like any book, what would you pick?"

### 4.2 Techniques for Voice Matching

**Transcript-first methodology:**
"Starting in a transcript means starting in the real speaking voice of clients. If the client uses a particular metaphor or defaults to a certain word to describe something, it's right there in front of the ghostwriter." Everything written has a foundation in the client's natural speaking voice.

**Linguistic dissection:**
Ghostwriters analyze:
- **Diction and syntax** -- word choices and sentence structures
- **Sentence length preferences** -- short/declarative vs. long/winding
- **Speech tendencies** -- regional idioms, industry jargon, filler words
- **Metaphor preferences** -- whether they lean toward metaphor or plain facts
- **Topic navigation** -- whether they meander between topics or stay focused
- **Emphasis techniques** -- how they make a point or diffuse tension

**Voice documentation:**
Some ghostwriters maintain detailed spreadsheets tracking:
- Average words per sentence
- Paragraph length patterns
- Transition preferences
- Favorite expressions and verbal tics
- Characteristic analogies and reference points
- Topics they return to repeatedly ("signature fixations")

**The "best friend test":**
The gold standard for voice matching: "If their best friend read it, they'd never suspect it was penned by another."

### 4.3 Maintaining Authenticity While Writing as Someone Else

**The balancing act:**
"Use the subject's own words as the foundation of the draft and then take time to polish it to a professional standard, so the finished product should sound like the subject could have written it, assuming they had the ghostwriter's experience."

**Streamline, don't sanitize:**
Good ghostwriters streamline their client's "stream of consciousness" while preserving personal flair -- adding necessary context and nuance for reader comprehension while cutting unnecessary material.

**Iterative refinement process:**
- Submit sample passages early for client feedback
- "Matching the author's authentic voice generally doesn't happen until the manuscript is complete and at the editing stage"
- Rewrites are part of the process, not a failure
- The client must be able to "hear themselves" in the words

**Neutrality discipline:**
Ghostwriters must separate personal opinions from the client's perspective, staying "objective when writing viewpoints you disagree with" and focusing on clarity of the client's message.

**Building rapport for authentic capture:**
- Start with easy questions, progress to sensitive topics
- Use memory triggers (old photos, artifacts) to open personal stories
- Go off-script when prepared questions aren't working
- Demonstrate empathy and non-judgmental responses
- "Bottle that energy" -- capture not just words but the emotional quality of how the client presents themselves

## 5. Research on "AI Slop" Characteristics

### 5.1 Common AI Writing Crutches

**Structural crutches:**
- Numbered lists and bullet points for everything
- Subheadings that follow a predictable pattern
- Opening paragraphs that restate the question
- Closing paragraphs that summarize what was just said
- The "three-example" pattern (always exactly three supporting points)
- Parallel construction in every list item

**Vocabulary crutches:**
- Defaulting to formal, academic vocabulary where simple words would suffice
- "Utilize" instead of "use," "leverage" instead of "take advantage of"
- Consistent selection of Latinate vocabulary over Anglo-Saxon alternatives
- Generic modifiers: "significant," "important," "crucial," "essential"

**Rhetorical crutches:**
- Restating the premise before answering
- Providing balanced "on the other hand" perspectives on everything
- Ending every section with a summary or takeaway
- Beginning with broad context before getting to the specific point

### 5.3 Why AI Text "Feels" Different Even When Grammatically Correct

**The Linguistic Uncanny Valley:**
Multiple researchers have identified a phenomenon parallel to the visual uncanny valley. AI text is "grammatically flawless yet emotionally hollow." The writing "triggers an instinctive discomfort" because it "looks human but something crucial is missing."

**Root causes of the "wrongness":**

1. **Statistical optimization vs. meaning generation:**
   LLMs predict "statistically probable next tokens rather than generate meaning." They are "rewarded for producing something that fits the shape of an answer, not something anchored to reality." AI "doesn't model the reader; it models the statistical probability of the next word."

2. **Absence of three critical human elements:**
   - **Risk-taking:** Humans express strong, sometimes reckless opinions. Models play it safe.
   - **Authentic imperfection:** Humans include "awkward phrasing, unnecessary details, and sharp edges that paradoxically make writing feel real."
   - **Lived experience:** Without genuine experiences, AI "cannot produce authentic voice -- no foundation for conviction exists."

3. **Missing cognitive processes:**
   Human writing reflects "complex cognitive activities such as self-monitoring, discourse planning, and lexical retrieval." AI generates text "without pausing, self-monitoring, or strategic planning." Human refinements are "intentional"; AI refinements are "statistical, not intentional."

4. **Evolutionary detection mechanisms:**
   "Our brains are hardwired to detect deception as a survival mechanism. Perfect imitation signals potential threat." The "too perfect" quality of AI text actually triggers alarm rather than trust.

5. **The theory of mind deficit:**
   Humans write *to* an audience, modeling readers' reactions, knowledge, and potential misunderstandings. AI writes *at* an audience, optimizing for statistical probability. Readers can sense the difference: "It's talking at you, not to you."

**Research framework for measurement:**
Northeastern University researchers developed a taxonomy scoring "slop" across seven dimensions: density, relevance, factuality, bias, structure, coherence, and tone. Their study found approximately **35% of AI text samples** qualified as slop. The strongest predictors of slop judgments were Relevance, Density, and Tone. Critically, human assessment remains superior to computational approaches -- even GPT-5 achieved only kappa of approximately 0.01 when trying to identify slop.

### 6.3 Structural AI Tells

- Excessive comma-separated lists ("x, y, and z" in every other sentence)
- Identical section openings across different sections
- Uniform introductory formulas
- Every paragraph begins with a topic sentence and ends with a summary
- Em-dashes used as universal punctuation (the "ChatGPT dash")
- Present participial phrases as sentence openers ("Leveraging X, we can...")
- The "From X to Y" construction pattern
- Perfectly parallel list items (same grammatical structure for every bullet)
- Every question answered with a restatement of the question first
